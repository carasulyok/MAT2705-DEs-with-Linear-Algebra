    Section Course Information

    Course Description
    🔗
    The construction of mathematical models to address real-world problems has been one of the most important aspects of each of the branches of science. It is often the case that these mathematical models are formulated in terms of equations involving functions as well as their derivatives. Such equations are called differential equations. This course will demonstrate the usefulness of ordinary differential equations for modeling physical and other phenomena. Complementary mathematical approaches for their solution will be presented, including analytical methods, graphical analysis, and numerical techniques. The basic content of the course includes: first-order equations, mathematical models, matrix algebra, linear equations of second- and higher-order, nonlinear systems and phase plane analysis, and numerical methods.
    🔗
    Course Objectives
    🔗
    We will discuss Chapters 1-7 of the textbook, including some extra topics. After successful completion of the class, you should be able to do the following:
    1. Understand the concepts relating to the order and linearity of differential equations, analytic and computational techniques for solving differential equations, and application of differential equations.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Understand some fundamentals of linear algebra.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Apply your understanding of the concepts, formulas, and problem-solving procedures to thoroughly investigate relevant mathematical models of real-world phenomena.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Explain the concepts of linear systems, differential equation solution methods, and related ideas at a fundamental level, as well as how and why we use the solution techniques that we use.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Use technology and mathematical software, including Maple and MATLAB, to study mathematics.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Communicate mathematics effectively in both writing and orally.
      🔗
      🔗

    Course Topics
    🔗
    Broadly speaking, we will discuss the topics of first-order, one dimensional differential equations, linear algebra, systems of first-order differential equations, and higher-order differential equations.

    Exams
    🔗
    The style and grading of exams in this course may be different from anything you have experienced. We will use a method commonly referred to as “Grading for Growth” or “Mastery-Based Testing.” There will be three in-class Mastery Concept Days throughout the semester. Instead of using a percentage-based or points-based system on these exams, your grade will be determined by how well you display a mastery of specific mathematical objects called learning targets. The 19 learning targets for this course are listed below. Since there are no points, there is no partial credit. Students only receive credit when they display “mastery,” but there is a reassessment system in place that allows students to try a concept more than once to demonstrate understanding. You will have your first attempt at a concept on the week you submit the corresponding homework on myLab; Concept Days will be announced with at least one week notice.
    🔗
    Final Exam
    🔗
    The final exam is scheduled by the University and is noted on our Course Calendar. The final exam time will serve as the last retesting opportunity and will include all 19 learning targets that have not yet been mastered. If a student has already mastered all of the concepts, they will not need to take the final exam.

    Section Grading of Mastery-Based Exams

    Each Concept Day will include several learning targets. You will earn a mark for each target that indicates how well you demonstrated understanding of that target. There are three possible marks:
    🔗
    M - Mastered
    🔗
    Demonstrates understanding of the relevant target and no additional study or review is needed.
    🔗
    P - Progressing
    🔗
    Demonstrates partial understanding, but with fundamental error/misunderstanding or is incomplete. Needs review and revision.
    🔗
    X - Not assessable
    🔗
    Not enough work to determine mastery: an insubstantial attempt, too many errors to correct each individually, or uses an inaccurate method or tool.
    🔗
    🔗
    Once you have received an “M” on a learning target, you no longer have to attempt that learning target.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Calculating Exam Averages

    Each student’s exam average begins at 0 and is increased each time they master a learning target. Thus, you are progressively earning your grade over the course of the semester. The mastery of a single learning target will add 5.263% to the exam average so that a student who has mastered all 19 learning targets receives a 100% test average. If you receive a “P” on a learning target, you are advised to retest that concept. A grade of “P” will only add 2.5% to your test average per learning target, so no student will obtain a passing exam score with zero mastered concepts.
    🔗
    The primary source of extra attempts comes from the fact that once a concept has been tested, it will continue to appear on every subsequent Concept Day. Therefore, Concept Days get longer as the semester goes on and more learning targets are covered. There is not time to complete all 19 learning targets on a single day. Students are encouraged not to attempt all learning targets on every Concept Day, but to use their time wisely to attempt concepts they are most prepared for. There is no penalty for not attempting a learning target since it can be restested later. Also, once you have mastered a learning target, you can skip that problem. Each learning target is graded separately.
    🔗
    Fair Warning: This may be more complicated than the typical grading style you have become accustomed to over the years. That’s okay! If at any point in the semester you need clarification on how the system works or your standing in the course, I encourage you to come talk to or email me. The goal with this system is to provide you with more control over your grade with less stress and to reflect your effort and improvement (and negate the previously large impact of a single bad test day). It does not matter to me when in the semester you master a certain concept, just that you master it! Do not be discouraged if you do not master a learning target on the first attempt (or even the first few attempts). That’s normal! Failure, though often viewed negatively, is an integral part of the learning process. The way to truly learn a subject is to make mistakes, learn from them, and do better next time.
